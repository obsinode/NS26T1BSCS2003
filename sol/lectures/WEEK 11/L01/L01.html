1. A/B Testing – Definition & Purpose (Control Method)

Definition

A/B testing (also called control method or split‑testing) is a controlled experiment that compares two variants (A and B) to determine which performs better on a predefined metric. In business analytics, it is the gold standard for evaluating the causal impact of a change (e.g., a new marketing nudge, website redesign, or pricing strategy).

Explanation

Motivation: Without a controlled experiment, any observed change in behaviour could be due to external factors (seasonality, economic shifts). A/B testing isolates the effect of the intervention.

First principles: Users are randomly split into two statistically identical groups:
– Control group (A): experiences the existing system (no change).
– Test group (B): exposed to the new feature / nudge.
Both groups are observed over the same time period; any significant difference in the outcome (e.g., conversion rate, engagement score) is attributed to the change.

Intuition / Mental Model

Medical trial analogy: Just as a new drug is tested against a placebo using a treatment group and a control group, a business “treatment” (pink website, PayLater recommendation) is tested against the current “placebo”. Random assignment ensures that both groups are equivalent before the experiment.

Key Definitions

  • Control group: Subjects who do not receive the experimental treatment (they see the old website / original payment options).
  • Test (treatment) group: Subjects who do receive the new treatment (nudge, new default).
    • li>
  • Lift: The relative (or absolute) improvement in the target metric for the test group over the control group. \( \text{Lift} = \frac{\text{metric}_{\text{test}} - \text{metric}_{\text{control}}}{\text{metric}_{\text{control}}} \times 100\% \).
  • Statistical significance: A measure that the observed difference is unlikely to have occurred by random chance (typically p‑value < 0.05).

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

  • Misconception: “We can compare this month’s performance to last month (pre‑post) and call it A/B testing.”
    Why it’s wrong: Pre‑post designs cannot control for time‑based confounders (e.g., macroeconomic changes, holidays, competitor actions).
    Correction: True A/B testing runs test and control groups simultaneously, eliminating temporal bias.
    How to avoid: Always run the experiment in the same calendar period using random assignment.

Real‑World Application

E‑commerce checkout optimisation: An online retailer tests two versions of the payment page – one showing “Pay Later” prominently, the other showing standard credit/debit options – to measure impact on cart completion rates.

Key Takeaways

* A/B testing isolates causal effect of a change by comparing two parallel groups.
* Control group continues as usual; test group receives the treatment.
* The difference (lift) must be statistically significant before rolling out the change globally.
* Simultaneous execution avoids pitfalls of pre‑post analysis.

2. Experimental Design & Group Similarity Checks

Definition

Before analysing the outcome of an A/B test, we must verify that the assignment of users to test and control groups was balanced – meaning that the groups are statistically similar on all relevant pre‑experiment characteristics (e.g., past transaction behaviour, credit/debit usage, age, tenure).

Explanation

Why similarity matters: If the test group already had higher average transaction value before the experiment, any observed post‑test difference could be due to pre‑existing disparity, not the nudge.

Common approach (as used in PayBuddy): Compare average of key metrics (3‑month credit card spend, 3‑month debit card spend) between test and control using a pivot table. For a well‑designed experiment, these averages should be roughly equal.

Formulas / Formal Notation

Let \( \bar{X}_{\text{test}} \) and \( \bar{X}_{\text{control}} \) be the sample means for a pre‑experiment metric. Practical equivalence holds when \( |\bar{X}_{\text{test}} - \bar{X}_{\text{control}}| \) is small relative to the scale of the metric. In the PayBuddy case:

\[ \text{Control: avg credit (3m)} = 1719,\quad \text{Test: avg credit (3m)} = 1796 \]

Student’s t‑test or simple visual inspection confirms similarity.

Step‑by‑Step Procedure (using pivot tables)

  1. Select the dataset
    Include columns: group (test/control), pre‑trial credit usage, pre‑trial debit usage.
  2. Insert pivot table
    Rows = group; Values = AVERAGE(credit_3m) and AVERAGE(debit_3m).
  3. Compare the averages
    Verify that both groups are within a reasonable tolerance (no large divergence).
  4. Optional – additional dimensions
    Add age, tenure, or past engagement to ensure robustness (though correlated dimensions often behave similarly).

Diagrams / Tables

Example from AB Testing Working.pdf:

Group Avg Credit (3 months) Avg Debit (3 months)
Control (C) 1,719 3,434
Test (T) 1,796 slightly higher (≈3,460)

Interpretation: Groups are sufficiently similar; the experiment design is valid.

Key Takeaways

* Always check group equivalence on key behavioural metrics before analysing outcomes.
* In practice, selecting 2‑3 representative dimensions (e.g., credit/debit spend) is enough because correlated variables will also match.
* Unbalanced groups invalidate causal conclusions.

3. Measuring A/B Test Outcomes – Engagement Segment Migration

Definition

Instead of a single binary metric, customer engagement segments (Low, Medium, High, About‑to‑churn) show how behaviour evolves. The test’s success is measured by how many customers move from lower to higher engagement (or stay in high) compared to the control group.

Explanation

Motivation: A nudge (e.g., showing PayLater on mobile) aims to increase long‑term engagement, not just one transaction. Engagement transition matrices capture the effect.

Calculation method: Use a pivot table with old engagement segment as rows, new engagement segment as columns, and count of customers as values. Then filter by test/control group and compute row percentages.

Example results (from PayBuddy experiment)

  • About‑to‑churn → High: Control 32%, Test 59% → large positive lift.
  • High → High retention: Control 50%, Test 73% → test group customers stay engaged longer.
  • Low → Low (stuck): Control 53%, Test only 16% → nudge helps low‑engagement users move up.

Formulas / Formal Notation

For a given starting segment \( i \) and ending segment \( j \), the transition proportion for group \( g \) is:

\[ P_{ij}^{(g)} = \frac{\text{Count of customers moving from } i \text{ to } j \text{ in group } g}{\text{Total customers starting in } i \text{ in group } g} \]

The net positive mobility is the sum of probabilities moving to higher‑value segments minus those moving downward.

Diagrams / Tables

Control group transition (%) – about‑to‑churn:

→ Medium → High
68% 32%

Test group transition (%) – about‑to‑churn:

→ Medium → High
41% 59%

Absolute improvement in “High” destination = 27 percentage points.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

  • Misconception: “Any movement to higher engagement proves our nudge works.”
    Correction: Compare against the control group; natural organic improvements happen over time (e.g., 32% already moved to high without nudge). The lift (extra 27%) is the true causal effect.

Key Takeaways

* Use contingency tables (old vs new segment) to visualise behaviour change.
* Row percentages control for different starting group sizes.
* Significant lift in upward migration indicates the nudge is effective.

4. Statistical Significance & The Novelty Effect

Definition

Statistical significance quantifies the probability that an observed difference between test and control is due to the intervention rather than random variation. The novelty effect (or “Hawthorne effect”) occurs when an improvement stems purely from the fact that something new has been introduced, not from the intrinsic value of the change.

Explanation

After an A/B test, even if the test group shows higher engagement, we must ask:
1) Could this difference have arisen by chance? (hypothesis test).
2) Will the effect persist over time or fade once the novelty wears off?
In the PayBuddy case, the test group’s engagement uplift was large (e.g., 59% vs 32%), making random chance extremely unlikely. However, the novelty effect might still cause the lift to erode after a few months.

Formulas / Formal Notation

For two proportions \( p_{\text{test}} \) and \( p_{\text{control}} \) (e.g., % moving to High), a Z‑test is used:

\[ Z = \frac{p_{\text{test}} - p_{\text{control}}}{\sqrt{p(1-p)(\frac{1}{n_{\text{test}}} + \frac{1}{n_{\text{control}}})}}, \quad p = \frac{n_{\text{test}}p_{\text{test}} + n_{\text{control}}p_{\text{control}}}{n_{\text{test}} + n_{\text{control}}} \]

If \(|Z| > 1.96\), the difference is significant at the 5% level.

Real‑World Application

E‑commerce platforms run “holdout” tests lasting 4–8 weeks. If the treatment group’s lift remains significant after the first two weeks (and doesn’t taper), the feature is permanently rolled out.

Key Takeaways

* Always test for statistical significance before declaring a winner.
* Large effect sizes (e.g., 27 pp lift) are often significant even with moderate sample sizes.
* Novelty effect can inflate short‑term results; consider running the test for multiple cycles or analysing week‑by‑week trends.

5. What is Credit Risk Analysis? (PayBuddy Perspective)

Definition

Credit risk analysis is the process of evaluating the likelihood that a borrower will default on a loan (fail to repay the borrowed amount). For a “Buy Now Pay Later” (BNPL) company like PayBuddy, it involves assessing each credit application using credit scores, transaction history, engagement segments, and application amount to approve only those who are likely to repay.

Explanation

Why credit risk matters: PayBuddy lends without collateral. If a customer defaults, the entire loan amount is lost. At the same time, declining too many applications loses revenue and customer goodwill. The credit risk officer (Nikita Sharma) balances approval rate and default loss.

Key components: Credit score (internal or CIBIL-like), application amount, customer segment (high/medium/low engagement), decline reasons (compliance, eligibility, fraud risk, system issue).

Intuition / Mental Model

Bank teller analogy: A bank loan officer checks your credit history, income, and purpose of loan before saying “yes”. The credit risk analysis automates that decision using data and rules.

Key Definitions

  • Default: Failure to pay an EMI when due. In the dataset, default is binary (paid back = 0, default = 1).
  • Credit score (internal): A numerical score (e.g., 400–800) summarising a customer’s creditworthiness; higher is better.
  • CIBIL / external score: Centralised credit bureau score used by many lenders in India.
  • Reject inferencing: Techniques to estimate the performance of declined applicants using external data (since they never receive a loan).

Edge Cases & Corner Cases

  • No historical credit data: PayBuddy may rely on other signals – transaction frequency, device information, or tenure with the platform.
  • Compliance decline: Even with excellent credit score, a customer may be declined for being under 18 or having incomplete KYC.

Key Takeaways

* Credit risk is about predicting probability of default.
* A credit risk officer balances approval volume vs. losses.
* Internal scores, engagement level, and application amount are typical predictors.

6. Credit Risk Dataset – Detailed Explanation

Definition

The credit risk dataset contains every credit application (Buy Now Pay Later) over a three‑month period. Each row represents one application and includes customer information, application details, approval decision, decline reason (if any), and repayment outcome.

Columns & Explanation

Column Description
Customer ID Unique identifier for each customer.
Date of Application Day when the credit was requested (Jan – April).
Customer Segment Engagement level: High, Medium, Low (based on past behaviour).
Credit Score Internal risk score (higher = less risky).
Application Amount (₹ / $) Dollar value of the credit requested.
Approved/Declined Binary decision.
Decline Reason If declined: Compliance, Credit Risk, Eligibility, Fraud Risk, System Issue.
Paid Back / Default For approved loans: indicates whether the customer eventually repaid.

Common Decline Reasons

  • Compliance: KYC missing, age under 18, regulatory restrictions.
  • Credit risk: Internal or external credit score below threshold.
  • Eligibility: Does not meet company rules (e.g., less than 30 days on platform).
  • Fraud risk: Suspicious activity, unusual device/location.
  • System issue: Technical glitch during evaluation.

Data Quality Notes

For declined applications, “Paid Back / Default” is marked NA (no loan given). For approved applications, the field is binary (paid back / default). The dataset is simplified for education; real‑world data would include delinquency stages (30‑60‑90 days past due).

Key Takeaways

* Each column plays a role in the approval rule.
* Decline reasons help understand why customers are rejected.
* Default flag enables calculation of loss and model validation.

7. Monthly Trends – Approval Rate & Credit Score Analysis

Definition

Monitoring key risk metrics month over month reveals if the company is becoming more lenient (or stricter) and how the credit quality of applicants changes.

Analysis from PayBuddy data (Jan – Mar 2020)

Approval rate trend:
January: 48% approved, 52% declined.
February: 46% approved.
March: 58% approved → significant jump.
April: limited data (only two days).

Average credit score of approved applicants:
January: 670, February: 635, March: 599 → steady decline. This suggests the company lowered the credit score threshold to approve more customers.

Average credit score of declined applicants: remained roughly flat (~500-520), indicating that the overall applicant pool’s quality deteriorated slightly, but the main driver of higher approval was relaxed criteria.

Formulas / Formal Notation

Approval rate = \(\frac{\text{Number of approved applications}}{\text{Total applications}} \times 100\)
Average credit score for approved = \(\frac{\sum \text{credit score of approved}}{\text{count of approved}}\)

Visualisation recommendation

Line chart with two lines: approval rate (%) and average credit score (approved). A negative correlation (approval ↑ while score ↓) indicates riskier lending.

Key Takeaways

* A sudden increase in approval rate often stems from a rule change (lower score threshold).
* Declining credit score of approved customers signals elevated future default risk.
* Overall population credit score can also drop due to economic conditions (e.g., COVID) independently of company policy.

8. Monthly Trends – Application Amount (Ticket Size)

Definition

The application amount (loan size) is a critical risk factor: larger loans expose the company to higher loss if default occurs. Companies often enforce a maximum approved amount that increases gradually as they gain confidence.

Observed trends from PayBuddy

  • Average approved amount: Jan $73 → Feb $112 → Mar $150 → Apr $184 (upward).
  • Average declined amount: stayed flat ~$358 – meaning customers’ requested amounts did not change; only approval policy changed.
  • Maximum approved amount: Jan $90, Feb $130, Mar $180, Apr $242 → the company systematically raised the cap.

Formulas / Formal Notation

Let \( A_{\text{approved}}^{(m)} \) be the set of approved loan amounts in month \( m \). Then:
\[ \text{Avg approved amount}_m = \frac{1}{|A_{\text{approved}}^{(m)}|}\sum_{a \in A_{\text{approved}}^{(m)}} a \]
Max approved \( = \max(A_{\text{approved}}^{(m)}) \).

Implication for risk

Higher average loan amount combined with lower credit score leads to larger losses when defaults occur. This explains why losses increased month over month (see profit analysis).

Key Takeaways

* Rising approved amount while keeping income/score constant increases risk exposure.
* The gap between approved and declined amounts narrows over time, meaning the company is “pushing the envelope”.

9. Transaction‑level Profitability: Revenue, Loss, Profit

Definition

For every credit application, PayBuddy can compute:
Revenue = commission earned on the transaction if approved (3.5% of application amount).
Loss = entire application amount if the customer defaults (conservative assumption).
Profit = Revenue – Loss.

Assumptions & Formulas

\[ \text{Revenue} = \begin{cases} \text{Application Amount} \times 0.035, & \text{if Approved} \\ 0, & \text{if Declined} \end{cases} \]
\[ \text{Loss} = \begin{cases} \text{Application Amount}, & \text{if Approved and Default} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

\[ \text{Profit} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Loss} \]

Example from dataset

  • Transaction 1: Approved, amount $168, paid back → Revenue = $5.88, Loss = $0, Profit = $5.88.
  • Transaction 2: Approved, amount $109, default → Revenue = $3.82, Loss = $109, Profit = –$105.18.
  • Declined transaction: Revenue = 0, Loss = 0, Profit = 0 (customer may still pay via debit card, but model assumes worst case).

Step‑by‑Step (Excel) Implementation

  1. Create column `Revenue`: `=IF(approved_cell="Approved", app_amount*0.035, 0)`.
  2. Create column `Loss`: `=IF(AND(approved_cell="Approved", default_cell="Default"), app_amount, 0)`.
  3. Create column `Profit`: `=Revenue - Loss`.
  4. Use pivot tables to sum Profit by month or segment.

Key Takeaways

* Revenue is capped at 3.5% commission, while a default can wipe out the entire principal.
* Even a small default rate can erase all profits from many successful transactions.
* This simple model helps quantify the risk‑return trade‑off.

10. Monthly Profit / Loss Aggregation & Strategic Trade‑off

Definition

By summing profit across all transactions each month, we see whether the credit business is profitable or not and how losses evolve as approval policy becomes more aggressive.

Findings from PayBuddy analysis

  • January: Small profit (or low loss).
  • February: Loss increased.
  • March: Loss significantly higher despite higher revenue.
  • Reason: higher approval rate + larger average loan amounts + lower credit quality → more defaults in dollar terms.

Loss as percentage of revenue rose from single digits to over 400% (in the short April sample), indicating unsustainable risk.

Intuition / Mental Model

Gas pedal analogy: Approving more customers (especially with larger loans) is like pressing the accelerator; revenue grows, but default losses grow even faster if risk scoring is weak. The credit risk officer must find the “sweet spot”.

Formulas / Formal Notation

Monthly profit \( \Pi_m = \sum_{\text{transactions in month } m} (\text{Revenue}_t - \text{Loss}_t) \).
Loss ratio = \( \frac{\text{Total loss in month } m}{\text{Total revenue in month } m} \). A loss ratio > 1 means the business is cash‑negative on credit operations.

Key Takeaways

* Aggressive approval can lead to explosive growth in losses, even if revenue increases.
* Monitoring monthly profit/loss trends is essential to prevent insolvency.
* The long‑term customer engagement benefits must eventually outweigh short‑term losses.

11. Engagement Segment & Credit Approval Strategy

Observation

When analysing approval rates across engagement segments (High/Medium/Low), PayBuddy approved roughly half of the applications in each segment. That means high‑engaged customers were not treated preferentially – a missed opportunity because engaged customers are more valuable and have lower default risk.

Average approved amount by segment

  • High segment: avg approved amount $134, max $240.
  • Medium segment: similar to high.
  • Low segment: avg approved amount $110, max $180.

While the approved amount differed, the approval rate did not. Recommendation: increase approval rate for high‑engagement customers to boost loyalty and revenue.

Why this matters

Credit risk is not only about credit score; engagement history is a strong proxy for future transactions. Companies should incorporate engagement into the approval model (higher weight for High segment).

Key Takeaways

* Approval rates alone can be misleading – look at amounts and default rates per segment.
* High‑engagement customers should get higher approval rates, not just higher loan caps.
* Segment‑based policies improve customer lifetime value and reduce adverse selection.

12. Reject Inferencing & Regulatory Constraints

Definition

Reject inferencing is the practice of estimating how declined applicants would have performed if they had received a loan, using external data (e.g., CIBIL scores of that population) or by running small‑scale shadow approvals. It helps refine credit models without taking excessive risk.

Motivation

If you never approve borderline applicants, you never learn whether they would have paid back. This leads to overly conservative models and lost revenue. Reject inferencing provides a feedback loop.

Regulatory considerations

  • US (Fair Lending): Cannot use race, gender, age, or zip code as a proxy for discrimination.
  • India: Regulations are evolving but currently allow more flexibility. However, best practice is to avoid protected attributes.
  • PayBuddy must ensure that its credit model does not indirectly discriminate against any group.

Key Takeaways

* Reject inferencing closes the loop for declined applications.
* Regulatory compliance is essential when deploying credit models.
* Internal data (like engagement) can be used freely but must be fair.

13. Buy Now Pay Later Economics – Cost of Capital & Float

Definition

PayBuddy lends at 0% interest to the customer, but it has to pay its own cost of capital (e.g., 2‑5% borrowing rate). The gap is funded either by the company’s own capital or by using the float (customer wallet balances held with PayBuddy).

Explanation

Even if PayBuddy uses customer float (money sitting in wallets), that money could have earned interest in a bank deposit. Therefore, an opportunity cost exists. The only sustainable way to make BNPL profitable is to generate enough incremental transaction volume (and merchant commissions) to offset the cost of funds and default losses.

Formulas / Formal Notation

Let \( r_{\text{cost}} \) = effective cost of capital (borrowing or opportunity). Then the net profit contribution of a transaction is:
\[ \Pi_{\text{net}} = \text{Commission} - \text{Expected Default Loss} - r_{\text{cost}} \times \text{Loan Amount} \]

Strategic Takeaway

In the PayBuddy A/B test, the test group’s higher engagement (more transactions) must eventually create enough commission revenue to outweigh the extra risk and capital cost. The initial losses are an investment in customer loyalty.

Key Takeaways

* BNPL is not free; lenders pay interest or forego float income.
* Customer engagement (frequency of purchase) is the key profit driver.
* Short‑term losses are acceptable if long‑term lifetime value improves.

14. Using Pivot Tables for Credit Risk Diagnostics

Definition

Spreadsheet pivot tables are the primary tool used in the course to summarise credit risk data – e.g., computing approval rates by month, average credit scores by approval status, and total profit per period.

Common Pivot Table Setups

  • Approval rate by month: Rows = Date (month), Columns = Approved/Declined, Values = Count of Customer ID → then compute %.
  • Average credit score by month: Same rows/columns, but Values = Average of Credit Score.
  • Average application amount: Values = Average of Application Amount.
  • Monthly profit/loss: Rows = Date, Values = Sum of Profit.

Step‑by‑step for approval rate

  1. Insert pivot table using credit risk dataset.
  2. Drag `Date of application` to Rows (group by month).
  3. Drag `Approved/Declined` to Columns.
  4. Drag `Customer ID` to Values (Count).
  5. Compute %: add calculated field or copy pivot and divide approved count by total count.

Advantages over manual calculation

Pivot tables are fast, update automatically when data changes, and reduce human error when handling hundreds of rows.

Key Takeaways

* Pivot tables are essential for slicing credit data by time, segment, or decision.
* Always verify that aggregates (average, count) are appropriate for the question.
* Use formatting (percentages, decimals) to improve readability.

15. Why Did PayBuddy’s Losses Increase? (Root Cause)

Diagnosis

  1. Relaxed credit score threshold → approved lower‑quality borrowers.
  2. Increased maximum approved amount (from $90 to $242) → larger exposure per default.
  3. Overall applicant credit quality slightly declined (economic environment).

Together, these three factors caused the loss per month to rise faster than revenue, even though revenue also grew.

Trade‑off for the credit risk officer

If Nikita keeps approving more people with higher amounts, short‑term losses will continue to increase. She can either:
– Tighten criteria (lower approval rate, smaller loan caps) → reduces losses but stunts growth.
– Keep looser criteria but improve collection mechanisms and hope that engagement lift compensates later.
– Adopt a more nuanced model: approve high‑engagement customers more often but keep strict risk rules for low‑engagement ones.

Real‑world parallel

Many fintechs (e.g., Affirm, Klarna) initially report losses as they scale, then adjust underwriting models to become profitable after gaining market share.

Key Takeaways

* Loss growth is not necessarily a sign of failure; it can reflect deliberate expansion.
* However, without corresponding growth in high‑quality repeat customers, losses become unsustainable.

16. Week 11 Synthesis – A/B Testing meets Credit Risk

Bridge between topics

Both A/B testing and credit risk analysis rely on controlled comparison and data‑driven decision making:

  • A/B testing isolates the effect of a nudge on engagement – which in turn affects a customer’s creditworthiness (more engaged → lower risk).
  • Credit risk analysis uses historical data to predict default, and A/B testing can validate whether offering BNPL to previously risky segments improves their repayment behaviour.

Final Checklist for Exam / Assignment

  • For an A/B test: Verify group similarity, compute engagement transition percentages, test statistical significance, watch for novelty effect, calculate lift.
  • For credit risk: Understand each dataset column, compute approval rates and average credit scores per month, calculate revenue (3.5% commission), loss (full amount on default), profit = revenue – loss, and aggregate by month/segment.
  • Business insight: Always balance growth (approval rate) with risk (default loss). Use segment‑specific strategies.
